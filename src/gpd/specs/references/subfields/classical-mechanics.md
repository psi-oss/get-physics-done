# Classical Mechanics

## Core Methods

**Detailed protocols:** For step-by-step calculation protocols, see `references/protocols/classical-mechanics.md`, `references/protocols/hamiltonian-mechanics.md`, `references/protocols/electrodynamics.md`, `references/protocols/numerical-computation.md`, `references/protocols/order-of-limits.md`.

**Lagrangian Mechanics:**

- Lagrangian L = T - V; generalized coordinates q_i and velocities q_i-dot
- Euler-Lagrange equations: d/dt (dL/dq_i-dot) - dL/dq_i = 0
- Constraints: holonomic (reduce DOF) vs non-holonomic (cannot be integrated); Lagrange multipliers for constrained systems
- Noether's theorem: continuous symmetry → conserved quantity (time translation → energy, spatial translation → momentum, rotation → angular momentum)
- Gauge freedom: L and L + dF/dt give identical equations of motion

**Hamiltonian Mechanics:**

- Legendre transform: H(q, p) = sum_i p_i q_i-dot - L; p_i = dL/dq_i-dot
- Hamilton's equations: dq_i/dt = dH/dp_i, dp_i/dt = -dH/dq_i
- Phase space: 2n-dimensional space (q_1, ..., q_n, p_1, ..., p_n)
- Poisson brackets: {f, g} = sum_i (df/dq_i dg/dp_i - df/dp_i dg/dq_i)
- Canonical transformations: preserve the symplectic structure; generated by F(q, Q), F(q, P), F(p, Q), or F(p, P)
- Hamilton-Jacobi equation: H(q, dS/dq, t) + dS/dt = 0; S is the action, complete solution gives all trajectories

**Canonical Perturbation Theory:**

- Time-dependent perturbation: H = H_0(J) + epsilon H_1(theta, J) in action-angle variables
- Averaging: eliminate fast angle variables to get secular evolution of slow actions
- Near-identity canonical transformations: new variables differ from old by O(epsilon)
- Small denominators: resonances where n.omega = 0 cause divergences; Kolmogorov's solution (KAM theory)
- Lie series: systematic construction of canonical transformations order by order

**Hamilton-Jacobi Theory:**

- Principal function S(q, alpha, t): solves H(q, dS/dq, t) + dS/dt = 0
- Characteristic function W(q, alpha): S = W - Et for time-independent H
- Action-angle variables: J_i = (1/2pi) oint p_i dq_i (area enclosed in phase space); theta_i = dW/dJ_i evolves linearly: theta_i(t) = omega_i t + theta_i(0)
- Separation of variables: when H = H_1(q_1, p_1) + H_2(q_2, p_2) + ..., the HJ equation separates into independent ODEs

## Specialized Topics

### KAM Theory (Kolmogorov-Arnold-Moser)

- **Statement:** For a nearly integrable Hamiltonian H = H_0(J) + epsilon H_1(theta, J), most invariant tori of H_0 survive the perturbation, provided: (a) epsilon is sufficiently small, (b) the frequency vector omega(J) is sufficiently irrational (Diophantine condition: |n.omega| >= C/|n|^tau for all integer vectors n).
- **Consequences:** Phase space has a mixed structure — KAM tori (regular, quasi-periodic motion) coexist with chaotic regions (where tori have broken). The fraction of phase space that is chaotic grows with epsilon.
- **Practical criterion:** Estimate epsilon from the perturbation strength. If epsilon > epsilon_c (the critical perturbation strength for torus breakdown), KAM tori are destroyed for that action. epsilon_c is not analytically computable in general — numerical methods (Greene's residue criterion, frequency analysis) are needed.

### Lyapunov Exponents

- **Definition:** lambda = lim_{t->inf} (1/t) ln(|delta x(t)| / |delta x(0)|) where delta x(t) is the separation between nearby trajectories. lambda > 0 indicates chaos (exponential divergence of trajectories).
- **Numerical computation:** Evolve a trajectory and a nearby tangent vector simultaneously. Periodically renormalize the tangent vector to avoid overflow. The Lyapunov exponent is the time-averaged logarithmic growth rate.
- **Lyapunov spectrum:** For an n-dimensional phase space, there are n Lyapunov exponents lambda_1 >= lambda_2 >= ... >= lambda_n. Hamiltonian systems have a symmetric spectrum: lambda_i = -lambda_{2n+1-i} (Lyapunov exponents come in +/- pairs). Sum of all Lyapunov exponents = 0 for volume-preserving flows (Liouville's theorem).
- **Lyapunov time:** t_Lyap = 1/lambda_max sets the predictability horizon. Predictions beyond ~5 t_Lyap are meaningless for chaotic systems.

### Symplectic Integrators

- **Why symplectic?** Hamiltonian flow preserves the symplectic 2-form dp wedge dq. Non-symplectic integrators (Euler, standard RK4) do not preserve this structure, leading to secular energy drift that grows linearly with time. Symplectic integrators have bounded energy error (no secular drift).
- **Stormer-Verlet (leapfrog):**
  p_{n+1/2} = p_n - (dt/2) dH/dq(q_n)
  q_{n+1} = q_n + dt dH/dp(p_{n+1/2})
  p_{n+1} = p_{n+1/2} - (dt/2) dH/dq(q_{n+1})
  Second-order, symplectic, time-reversible. The workhorse of molecular dynamics and celestial mechanics.
- **Higher-order methods:** Yoshida splitting and Forest-Ruth methods give 4th and 6th order symplectic integrators by composing leapfrog steps with different time steps (some negative).
- **When NOT symplectic:** Dissipative systems (friction, radiation reaction) do not have a symplectic structure. Use standard integrators. Systems with velocity-dependent forces may require special treatment.

### Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos

- **Bifurcation theory:** As a control parameter changes, the qualitative structure of phase space changes at bifurcation points. Types: saddle-node (creation/annihilation of fixed points), pitchfork (symmetry breaking), Hopf (onset of oscillations), period-doubling cascade (route to chaos).
- **Strange attractors:** Dissipative chaotic systems have attractors with fractal dimension. The Lorenz attractor (d ~ 2.06), Henon attractor (d ~ 1.26), Rossler attractor. Fractal dimension from box-counting or correlation dimension.
- **Poincare sections:** Reduce a continuous-time system to a discrete map by recording intersections with a surface of section. Fixed points of the map correspond to periodic orbits of the flow. The map reveals the mixed regular-chaotic structure of phase space.
- **Ergodicity and mixing:** Ergodic systems explore all of phase space (time average = phase space average). Mixing is stronger: correlations decay in time. KAM systems are NOT ergodic (invariant tori prevent full exploration). Fully chaotic systems (Sinai billiard, Arnold cat map) are both ergodic and mixing.

## Validation Strategies

**Energy Conservation:**

- For autonomous Hamiltonian systems: H(q(t), p(t)) = H(q(0), p(0)) for all t
- Check: relative energy drift |H(t) - H(0)| / |H(0)| should be bounded (symplectic) or grow linearly (non-symplectic)
- For symplectic integrators: energy oscillates around the true value with amplitude O(dt^p) but no secular drift

**Phase Space Volume (Liouville's Theorem):**

- Hamiltonian flow preserves phase space volume: det(dX(t)/dX(0)) = 1 for all t
- Check: evolve a cloud of initial conditions and monitor the determinant of the tangent map
- Violation indicates non-Hamiltonian forces or numerical error

**Symplectic Structure:**

- Symplectic 2-form: sum_i dp_i wedge dq_i is preserved
- Check: compute the symplectic matrix J = dX(t)/dX(0) and verify J^T Omega J = Omega where Omega is the standard symplectic matrix
- For symplectic integrators: this should be satisfied to machine precision

**Known Integrable Systems:**

- Kepler problem: verify elliptical orbits, correct period T = 2pi a^{3/2} / sqrt(GM), Runge-Lenz vector conserved
- Harmonic oscillator: verify sinusoidal motion, correct frequency, energy conservation
- Toda lattice: verify soliton solutions propagate without distortion

**Lyapunov Exponent Convergence:**

- lambda_max should converge as integration time increases
- For regular orbits: lambda -> 0 (power-law divergence, not exponential)
- For chaotic orbits: lambda > 0 and converges to a finite value
- Check symmetry of Lyapunov spectrum for Hamiltonian systems

## Common Pitfalls

- **Using non-symplectic integrators for long-time Hamiltonian dynamics.** RK4 introduces O(dt^4) energy error per step that accumulates secularly. After N steps, the energy error is O(N * dt^4) = O(T * dt^3). Symplectic integrators have bounded error O(dt^p) regardless of T.
- **Confusing chaos with numerical error.** Sensitive dependence on initial conditions means that individual trajectories are unpredictable after the Lyapunov time. But statistical properties (Lyapunov exponents, fractal dimension, invariant measure) are well-defined. Verify these, not individual trajectories.
- **Ignoring KAM theory for near-integrable systems.** Not all orbits are chaotic in a perturbed integrable system. KAM tori survive for sufficiently irrational frequency ratios. Phase space is a fractal mixture of regular and chaotic regions. Averaging over initial conditions without distinguishing regular from chaotic gives misleading results.
- **Wrong constraint forces.** Lagrange multipliers give the constraint force. A common error is applying constraints that are incompatible with the equations of motion, or failing to differentiate the constraint twice to get the constraint force.
- **Singular Lagrangians.** When det(d^2L/dq_i-dot dq_j-dot) = 0 (e.g., gauge theories, reparametrization-invariant systems), the Legendre transform to the Hamiltonian is singular. Dirac's constraint analysis is required to identify the true degrees of freedom.
- **Missing non-holonomic constraints.** Rolling without slipping, skates on ice, and other velocity-dependent constraints cannot be incorporated via Lagrange multipliers in the usual way. The d'Alembert-Lagrange principle or vakonomic mechanics may give different results — the physical choice depends on the problem.

## Methodology Decision Tree

```
What type of classical mechanics problem?
├── Exact analytical solution
│   ├── Integrable? → Action-angle variables, Hamilton-Jacobi equation
│   ├── Symmetry? → Noether theorem, reduction by symmetry group
│   └── Perturbative? → Canonical perturbation theory, Lie transforms
├── Stability analysis
│   ├── Fixed point? → Linearize, eigenvalues of Jacobian
│   ├── Periodic orbit? → Floquet analysis, Poincaré map
│   └── Global? → Lyapunov exponents, KAM analysis
├── Long-time integration
│   ├── Hamiltonian? → Symplectic integrator (Störmer-Verlet, Yoshida)
│   ├── Dissipative? → Standard ODE solver (RK45, DOPRI853)
│   └── Constrained? → SHAKE/RATTLE or differential-algebraic solver
└── Many-body
    ├── N small (< 10)? → Direct integration
    ├── N moderate (< 10^6)? → Barnes-Hut tree code
    └── N large? → Particle-mesh or FMM (Fast Multipole Method)
```
