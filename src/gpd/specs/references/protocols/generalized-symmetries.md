# Generalized Symmetries Protocol

Generalized symmetries extend the usual global-symmetry paradigm by replacing local conserved charges with topological defects acting on extended operators. The stable core of the subject is higher-form symmetry, its background fields, gauging, and anomalies. Higher-group and non-invertible structures are important extensions, but they should be introduced only when their extra data are explicit.

## Step 1: Identify the Symmetry as a Topological Operator

1. **Start from charged operators, not just the Lagrangian.** A `p`-form symmetry acts on `p`-dimensional operators and is generated by codimension-`(p+1)` topological defects.
2. **Classify the symmetry type:** ordinary 0-form, higher-form, higher-group, or non-invertible.
3. **Check operator support:** Wilson lines, 't Hooft lines, surface operators, domain walls, or other extended defects must be explicitly identified.
4. **Distinguish genuine from non-genuine operators:** if an operator can end on dynamical matter, it is not a genuine charged operator and the corresponding higher-form symmetry is broken or absent.
5. **Remember the abelian constraint:** invertible higher-form symmetries with `p > 0` are abelian. If a claim looks non-abelian, you are likely dealing with a higher-group or non-invertible structure instead.

## Step 2: Introduce Background Fields and Gauging Correctly

1. **Background degree:** A `p`-form global symmetry couples to a `(p+1)`-form background gauge field.
2. **Continuous case:** Track the conserved `(p+1)`-form current and the associated topological defect. Do not force a Noether-current derivation if the symmetry is discrete.
3. **Discrete case:** Identify the defect fusion rules and the extended operators it links. Discrete higher-form symmetries often appear through center symmetry or topological order.
4. **Gauging:** Gauging a generalized symmetry means summing over the appropriate background fields or introducing the corresponding dynamical higher-gauge fields. State exactly what is being gauged.
5. **Anomalies:** 't Hooft anomalies are obstructions to gauging and must be expressed through the background fields, inflow terms, or defect braiding data.

## Step 3: Use Canonical Diagnostics Before Making General Claims

1. **Maxwell theory:** 4d Maxwell theory carries electric and magnetic 1-form symmetries acting on Wilson and 't Hooft lines.
2. **Yang-Mills center symmetry:** Pure `SU(N)` Yang-Mills has a discrete `Z_N` 1-form center symmetry acting on Wilson lines in representations with non-trivial center charge.
3. **Matter breaks symmetry:** Dynamical fundamental matter explicitly breaks the corresponding center 1-form symmetry by screening Wilson lines.
4. **Phase diagnostics:** Use line/surface-operator behavior, center symmetry, and topological defects to diagnose confinement, deconfinement, or topological order. Do not translate every statement back into a local order parameter.
5. **Condensed-matter/topological-order viewpoint:** Defect algebra and extended operators can diagnose phases that lie outside the ordinary Landau paradigm.

## Step 4: Handle Breaking, Goldstones, and Defect Endings Carefully

1. **Spontaneous breaking of higher-form symmetry** is diagnosed through the behavior of charged extended operators, not through a local order parameter.
2. **Gapless gauge fields as Goldstones:** In standard examples, a spontaneously broken higher-form symmetry can give a gapless gauge field as the low-energy mode.
3. **Explicit breaking by new dynamical objects:** Adding charged matter or dynamical extended defects can break a higher-form symmetry by allowing previously genuine operators to end.
4. **Do not confuse gauge redundancy with generalized global symmetry.** Gauge symmetry is a redundancy of description; generalized global symmetry acts on physical extended operators.

## Step 5: Treat Higher-Group and Non-Invertible Structures as Data-Rich Extensions

1. **Higher-group symmetry:** Use this language only when ordinary and higher-form background fields transform together, typically with an explicit Postnikov class or equivalent mixing data.
2. **Non-invertible symmetry:** Claim this only when the symmetry defects are topological and obey a non-group-like fusion algebra. A duality is not automatically a non-invertible symmetry.
3. **SymTFT:** Symmetry topological field theory is a useful organizational tool for symmetries and anomalies, especially for non-invertible cases, but it is not the default calculational language unless a concrete construction is given.
4. **What is mature:** Higher-form symmetries, background fields, gauging, center symmetry, and many anomaly statements are standard.
5. **What is still frontier-heavy:** Broad non-invertible classifications, categorical actions in higher dimensions, and full SymTFT reconstructions should be labeled as advanced or active-research material.

## Step 6: Verification Checklist

| Check | Method | Catches |
|-------|--------|---------|
| Operator dimension | Verify that the symmetry acts on `p`-dimensional operators for a claimed `p`-form symmetry | Misidentified symmetry type |
| Topologicality | Deform the defect without crossing charged insertions and verify the result is unchanged | Non-topological objects mislabeled as symmetry generators |
| Background degree | Couple to a `(p+1)`-form background field | Wrong background-field structure |
| Abelian property | For invertible `p > 0` symmetries, verify the fusion/action is abelian | Impossible non-abelian higher-form claim |
| Genuine operator test | Check whether the charged extended operator can end on dynamical degrees of freedom | Hidden explicit breaking |
| Gauging/anomaly consistency | Verify gauging is allowed only when anomaly data vanish or are canceled by inflow | Illegal gauging or missing anomaly |
| Center-symmetry screening | In gauge theory, verify whether matter content screens Wilson lines | Wrong statement about confinement/deconfinement symmetry |
| Higher-group data | If a higher-group is claimed, write the mixed background-field transformation explicitly | Vague higher-group language with no structure |
| Non-invertible fusion | If non-invertible symmetry is claimed, write the defect fusion rule or gauging construction | Labeling any duality or topological order as non-invertible symmetry |

## Common LLM Errors in Generalized-Symmetry Work

1. **Confusing gauge redundancy with generalized global symmetry.**
2. **Coupling a `p`-form symmetry to the wrong degree of background field.**
3. **Claiming a non-abelian invertible higher-form symmetry.**
4. **Ignoring the distinction between genuine and non-genuine extended operators.**
5. **Using local order-parameter language where extended operators are the real diagnostics.**
6. **Calling any duality or topological defect a non-invertible symmetry without fusion data.**
7. **Treating SymTFT as mandatory or fully settled in contexts where it is only a useful advanced framework.**
